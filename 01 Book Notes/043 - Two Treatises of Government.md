# 043 - Two Treatises of Government

## 1. Why It Matters

*Two Treatises of Government* is one of the foundational texts of modern liberal political thought. Locke argues that legitimate government exists to protect natural rights rather than to embody absolute power. In doing so, he offers one of the most influential accounts of consent, property, limited government, and the right of resistance.

The book matters because it helps establish a new understanding of politics in which authority is justified by service to persons who are naturally free and equal. Much of later constitutionalism, liberalism, and democratic theory is unimaginable without it.

## 2. Place in the Canon

If [[042 - Leviathan]] gives the modern state a powerful defense, Locke provides one of the canonical arguments for limiting that state. He accepts the language of the state of nature and consent, but he arrives at very different conclusions about political legitimacy.

The book belongs in the canon because it links early modern debates about sovereignty to later struggles over rights, representation, revolution, and constitutional order. It is not only a philosophical text but a foundational document in the political imagination of the modern West.

## 3. Overview

The *Two Treatises* consists of two distinct but related works. The First Treatise is largely polemical: Locke attacks Sir Robert Filmer's theory that political authority descends from paternal and biblical authority. Locke's point is that political power cannot rest on sacred patriarchy or inherited domination.

The Second Treatise develops Locke's positive argument. Human beings in the state of nature are free and equal, governed by a natural law that forbids harming others in their life, liberty, and possessions. Political society arises when individuals consent to form a common authority capable of impartially protecting these rights.

Locke's theory of property is central. Individuals own themselves, and when they mix their labor with the world, they can appropriate property, provided enough and as good is left for others. Government exists not to create rights from nothing, but to secure rights that precede it.

Because government is fiduciary rather than absolute, it can lose legitimacy. When rulers violate the trust placed in them, the people retain a right to resist and replace them. This makes the book a defense not only of political order but of legitimate revolution.

## 4. Structure and Composition

The work is divided into two treatises:

1. **First Treatise**: a sustained refutation of patriarchal absolutism, especially Filmer.  
2. **Second Treatise**: Locke's constructive account of the state of nature, natural law, property, consent, political society, legislative power, and the right of revolution.

The first clears away an older doctrine of divine-right authority. The second builds a new account of political legitimacy from freedom, equality, and consent.

## 5. Core Idea

The core idea of the book is that government is legitimate only when it protects the natural rights of free and equal persons. Political authority is not original and absolute; it is delegated and conditional. Power is held in trust for the governed.

This produces a distinctly liberal picture of politics. Society does not exist for the glory of the ruler, and individuals do not exist as raw material for the state. Rather, political institutions are justified insofar as they preserve rights, adjudicate disputes, and secure the common good without destroying liberty.

## 6. Key Concepts

### Natural Equality

Locke begins from the premise that no one is naturally born the ruler of others. Human beings are by nature equal in moral status, and political hierarchy requires justification rather than assumption.

### Natural Rights

Life, liberty, and property are central goods protected by natural law. These are not gifts of government but claims that government must recognize and defend.

### Consent

Political authority becomes legitimate through consent. For Locke, consent need not mean permanent direct approval of every law, but it means that government rests on the authorization of the governed rather than pure force.

### Property and Labor

Locke's labor theory of property argues that individuals can appropriate from nature through work. This becomes one of the most influential and disputed parts of the text, linking personhood, effort, and ownership.

### Right of Resistance

Because government is a trust, rulers who violate that trust can be resisted. Locke thus provides a theory of revolution compatible with political order.

## 7. Major Themes

### Liberty Under Government

Locke rejects both anarchy and absolutism. The goal is lawful freedom, not power without restraint and not obedience without rights.

### Legitimate Authority

Authority must be justified through its purpose and source. The mere fact of rule does not make rule rightful.

### Moral Limits on Power

Political institutions are necessary, but they are morally bounded. This idea becomes central to constitutional thought.

### Property and Personhood

Property is not treated merely as wealth. It is tied to labor, self-ownership, and the independence needed for free life.

## 8. Major Questions

- What makes government legitimate?
- Are human beings naturally free and equal?
- What rights exist prior to political society?
- How does property become justly owned?
- Why should people consent to government?
- What are the limits of legislative power?
- When is rebellion justified?

## 9. Historical Context

Locke writes in the aftermath of the English Civil War, the Restoration, and the Glorious Revolution. These upheavals made the problem of sovereignty intensely practical. Could monarchy be limited? Could resistance be justified without collapsing into disorder?

The *Two Treatises* is closely associated with the constitutional settlement emerging from the Glorious Revolution of 1688–89. Although it is more than a partisan tract, it clearly speaks to the defense of limited government against absolutist claims.

The growth of commercial society also matters. Property, labor, exchange, and civil order were becoming more central to political life. Locke's theory reflects a world in which political legitimacy increasingly intertwines with social independence and economic relations.

The book belongs to a moment when older hereditary models of authority were losing their unquestioned force and new constitutional forms were being imagined.

## 10. Intellectual Context

Locke stands in dialogue with Hobbes, natural law theory, Protestant political thought, and emerging commercial modernity. He retains the contract framework but rejects Hobbes' severe state of nature and near-absolute sovereignty.

He also anticipates later Enlightenment political thought. Many ideas later associated with constitutionalism, representative government, and rights discourse receive one of their most durable formulations here.

## 11. Influence and Legacy

The influence of the *Two Treatises* on Anglo-American political thought is immense. The language of natural rights, consent, and resistance deeply shaped constitutional liberalism and revolutionary discourse, especially in the eighteenth century.

The book strongly influenced figures and texts later represented in the vault, including [[054 - Common Sense]] and broader revolutionary traditions. Its account of government as a trust remains central to liberal democratic theory.

It also shaped debates far beyond its admirers. Critics of liberalism, empire, and property relations often begin by confronting Lockean assumptions. In that sense, the book is foundational both to modern liberalism and to modern critiques of liberalism.

## 12. Criticism, Tensions, or Limits

One major tension lies in Locke's treatment of property. His theory links ownership to labor, but critics argue that it can justify inequality and exclusion on a scale the text does not adequately restrain.

Another criticism concerns consent. In actual political life, few people explicitly consent to the governments under which they live. Locke therefore relies on tacit consent in ways that later readers sometimes find weak or overly convenient.

There is also a historical tension between universal rights language and the unequal realities of empire, class, and exclusion in Locke's world. The gap between principle and historical practice remains one of the central difficulties in reading the text.

## 13. Modern Relevance

The book remains central wherever people debate constitutional limits, individual rights, political legitimacy, and the right to resist abusive government. Its framework still shapes public life, even for those who have never read it.

It also remains relevant because it crystallizes enduring liberal assumptions about freedom, property, and the role of the state. Modern political theory continues either to defend, revise, or contest the world Locke helped articulate.

## 14. Memorable Quote

> “where there is no law, there is no freedom.”

This line captures Locke's rejection of the false choice between liberty and political order. Freedom is not lawlessness; it is security under just law rather than arbitrary domination.

## 15. In One Sentence

Locke's *Two Treatises of Government* argues that legitimate government rests on consent and exists to protect the natural rights of free and equal persons.
